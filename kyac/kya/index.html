Mission Statement

Our Mission and Objectives at the KYAC are:

1. To organize and unite the population of Kurds and Kurdistani nations, citizens, students and youth in Canada towards working in unity for a Humanitarian objective;

2. To create a sense of belonging for the peoples and nations of Kurdistan, the youth and community;

3. To create leaders for tomorrow by providing numerous opportunities within KYAC and by providing various programs, events and training sessions to our members;

4. To assist, guide and lead the youth of our Kurdistani communities across Canada to a better, broader, more prosperous future by being a positive example;

5. Establish friendly relationships with all student associations and communities, especially those geographically close to Kurdistan and have lived within the Kurdistan borders for thousands of years;

6. To raise awareness to all aspects of Kurdish and Kurdistani issues across Canada and in our multicultural Canadian society, based on mutual understanding and respect;

7. To bring youth closer to their heritage, languages, communities and background and to instil pride;

8. To introduce people from different walks of life to our great and many nations of Kurdistan, its multi-multiculturalism, multi-faith, diverse and prosperous culture, music, languages, dialects, foods, mountains, etc;

9. To make you proud to know about Kurdistan, its many faiths, religions, languages, riches and diversity and to make you proud to be part of this organization; and

10. To raise awareness on all issues affecting the nations and peoples of Kurdistan both in Canada and in Kurdistan in a legal, humanitarian, charity and Canadian perspective. All activities of KYAC must conform to the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, at all times, without exception.

The history of Kurdish origins, linguistics, population and territory has been a source of contention for scholars, historiographers and politicians for centuries. Additionally, the division of the Kurdish people among the modern nation states of Iraq, Turkey, Syria and Iran and their struggle for national rights have been constant themes in recent literature. McDowall (2004) details the poignant events from the 16th to the 19th century when the Ottoman and Persian Empire strived to control the Kurds, whose tribal leaders would compete in turn for state recognition. During the 20th century, however, rapid political and economic transition and conflicting attempts by the Iranian, Iraqi, and Turkish governments, on the one hand, and by Turkish nationalists, on the other, have radically changed the conditions under which the struggle for Kurdish rights takes place. This history has, from the beginning, exposed the Kurds to an existence of exile and displacement until present day. From an essentialist point of view, the Kurdish Diaspora is arguably as old as the Kurdish nation itself.

Today, Kurds inhabit the mountainous region referred to as ‘Kurdistan,’ where the borders of Turkey, Syria, Iraq and Iran meet, although this does not refer to a political designation. The Kurds historically have always been a stateless people. During the short period between 1918-1925, they “lost their one opportunity at statehood, and found themselves apportioned as minorities in the new state system that replaced the Ottoman and Qajar Empires,” marking a defining moment for the Kurds.

Their dispersion and division among the four nation states and the continuous persecution and state-sanctioned limitations on cultural and ethnic expression make it difficult to agree on population census estimates provided by the home state statistics offices. The matter is complicated even more as different authors claim different figures which might also be based on biased interpretations. Regardless of historical roadblocks to calculating exact numbers, most literature provides estimation variances of between 25-30 million Kurds throughout the Middle East while estimates continue to increase (McDowall, 2004: 3; Hassanpour & Mojab, 2005). Much of the contentiousness stems from inconsistencies in historical records and lack of evidence for a common ancestry which often includes or excludes groups, not based on self-identification but based on given identities by external polities. This is because most historical records categorize Kurds as being descended from waves of indo-European tribes moving westwards across Iran, probably in the middle of the second millennium BCE (McDowall, 2004: 8). Lack of a uniform origin and fluctuating loyalties over the course of history, as they were handed down from one conqueror to another and juggled between nation-states, has rendered the Kurdish identity buoyant and incoherent at times.

Most self-identified Kurds and scholarship on Kurdish peoples will agree that, culturally, Kurds are distinct from the Arabs, Turks, and Persians, but are ethnically and linguistically closest to Persians (McDowall, 2004: 13-17; Hassanpour, 2003). The Kurds have been designated with an ethnic identity separate to that of their neighbours on several levels. The Kurds have identified themselves as a separate people, speaking an amalgam of different dialects, wearing different clothes, having a specific folklore and particular social customs. The Kurds speak several languages, depending on the part of Kurdistan from which they hail. The Kurds are also divided across religious lines. While most Kurds are Sunni Muslims, there are also other heretic religious communities of Alevis, Yezidis and Shia Muslims among the Kurds.
